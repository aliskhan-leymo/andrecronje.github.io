Disclaimer: These reviews are done as is from what is on display in the master branch of the repo’s made available. This review is not a comment on the overall project, scope, or success thereof. This was done as an educational review by me and any comments in the article are simply my opinion. It should not be used as any comment or advice on the project as a whole.

So, producers, or shall we say the centralized decentralized miners. I feel I’ve exhausted this topic already but let’s quickly talk about token value and distribution. People often like to compare centralized models to the top mining pools that control the networks, saying that they are just as centralized as centralized systems. For argument sake, I’m going to use Ethereum as an example.

Safe to say those top 4 pools “control” everything, those pool’s though are still compromised of a lot of individuals, and everytime a block reward or fee reward is given out it’s diluted amongst all of those individuals, while this might be centralization of mining power, it’s not centralization of ETH, as a value mechanism it is distributed across a significant amount of individuals, each with their own agenda, some hold, some sell, some buy more, there isn’t a centralized person that can control the fate of a token.

Now let’s look at EOS producers, 21 producers that are collecting all of the rewards. That’s a tiny amount of individuals that control a lot of power over the value of a token. 4.7% control over a tokens value is not something to be taken lightly.

To qualify, I’m not against these centralized decentralized models, I do believe they will hold value and I do believe it’s the easiest way to increase throughput, I just don’t want them categorized with true decentralized systems anymore. If you take geth and you streamline it a bit (and by streamline I mean remove valid user functionality like resubmitting transactions with a higher gas fee, or the byzantium fork) and you throw it in PoA on controlled hardware, you can get 10k TPS, then you are comparing a centralized decentralized system with a centralized decentralized system.

Anyway, moving on, not much else to see in the code.

Conclusion: The code is fine, difficult to read and a bit confusing to follow at first, but once you get a hang of their structure it ends up quite nice. I would have really liked to actually do a test on their throughput, but right now the node is frustrating to use, and since I didn’t save my auto generated password I’m locked out of the system in anycase. I really don’t want to do another install because of the sheer amount of time it takes.

A lot of time and effort has gone into this, although it feels unpolished, we will see how they compare with the other centralized decentralized models out there when they launch, I’ll give them a revisit in a few weeks to try to do an independent test again.

The ease of use definitely needs a bit more attention though. Also didn’t see any of the multi threading performance upgrades just yet. Still Blockchain 1.0 for now.
