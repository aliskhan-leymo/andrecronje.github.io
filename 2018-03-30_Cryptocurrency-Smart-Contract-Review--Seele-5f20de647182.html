Cryptocurrency Smart Contract Review: Seele

Disclaimer: These reviews are done as is from what is on display in the master branch of the repo’s made available. This review is not a comment on the overall project, scope, or success thereof. This was done as an educational review by me and any comments in the article are simply my opinion. It should not be used as any comment or advice on the project as a whole.

Seele contract is live, so we head on over to etherscan to check out their source, we could go to their github, but I prefer seeing the actual contract as saved, since contracts are immutable (can’t be changed or edited) I prefer it that way.

We start off with the Token first, and it starts off with the usual boiler plate, Onwable, Pausable (so tokens can be paused), SafeMath, ERC20Basic, ERC20 (when do we start seeing ERC777 by the way? I honestly can’t wait to launch my first ERC777), and StandardToken

Pet peeve item, that should be canClaim, but that’s irrelevant, let’s move on ;)

Guys, I know this is silly and pointless, but you are raising millions of dollars, don’t publish code with spelling mistakes! Still, that’s no reflection on the code, although it is a reflection on the attention to detail.

When addresses are allowed to claim then an array of recipients can be provided to claimTokens (anyone can do this), the list will be iterated over and the balances will be transferred from lockedBalances to balances, and lockedBalances will be set to 0. Not a fan, the system should have kept a list of all recipients and simply iterated over them, this implementation allows for manual mistakes, but let’s move on.

Ok, so the process goes like this, you start to receive your tokens via mint, this takes your address, an amount, and a boolean (isLock, this should have been isLocked, but anyway), if the total has not been reached and it is requested by the minter, then the recipient receives an amount in their lockedBalances. Later when claiming is allowed, you can claim your tokens. Only the owner can set the claiming state. The above function always returns a bool, was the return really necessary? A mistake here on isLock can be quite negative, I hope the minter pays very careful attention to what they are doing.

Let’s move on to CrowdSale contract

Some basic constants, sale duration will be 4 days, stage 1 will be 6 hour and have a 1 ether limit, stage 2 will be 12 hours and have a 2 ether limit, stage 3 I assume is unlimited. Minimum amount of 0.1 ether. Exchange rate will be 12500 (will see later where that comes into play)

Few more constants. Ok, so MAX_OPEN_SOLD is total supply (1 billion) * OPEN_SALE_STAKE (625) / DIVISOR_STAKE (10000), is only 6.25% of total tokens being made available for this sale? I thought it was 48.25%?

The minter gets 30%, and “Other” gets 63,75%, that’s a super tiny amount, but anyway.

I know this is really dumb and opinionated, and it does not reflect the quality of the code, and a lot of developers are not native English speakers, but if you are raising this kind of money, guys, do a spellcheck.

So, I’m nitpicking a lot here, but we have minerAddress, otherAddress, and then we have wallet, why not walletAddress? Where is the consistency?

Let’s get to the meat of it all. Can’t be sending from a contract, sorry PrimaBlock and ChronoLogic, gasprice has a maximum limit. You have to send more than the minimum of 0.1 and you have to be in the whitelist.

If now (current time) is after the startTime and now is less than the stage 1 time limit, the maximum is 1 ETH, if you have already sent something in, then your combined total is 1 ETH (to stop people from sending 0.9 a bunch of times), that secondary check is actually pointless, since in this case you could send in 0.9 and you could have already sent in 0.9, so it would bypass this check (but it’s covered further down)

Same setup for stage 2, and then we go to doBuy

Here we check if you would go over your limit, if you would then you are refunded the amount you sent, but the remainder is kept. Nice way of doing it.

There should be tokens available to be sold, if after costAnBuyTokens there is value available, the ether part immediately goes to the Seele wallet address, while the tokens minted are simply put in the locked address for now. NewSale event is emitted. If there are any ether left, it is returned to the sender.

Let’s have a look at costAndBuyTokens

Calculates how many tokens received based on the hardcoded exchange rate of 12500.

Conclusion: No major red flags here, straight forward code, bit sloppy, would expect more from a team raising as much money, but at least no massive flaws.
